1.a) What is an ideal voltage source?
[cite_start]

An ideal voltage source is a two-terminal source whose voltage remains constant regardless of the current drawn from its terminals[cite: 3].

[cite_start]

It has zero internal resistance ($R_i = 0$)[cite: 4].

1.b) Define KVL (Kirchhoff’s Voltage Law).
[cite_start]

Kirchhoff’s Voltage Law (KVL) states that the algebraic sum of all voltages (drops and rises) around any closed loop in a circuit must be zero[cite: 6].

[cite_start]

This law is based on the law of conservation of energy[cite: 7].

1.c) What is active power?
[cite_start]

Active power ($P$) is the actual power consumed by the load to perform useful work[cite: 9].

[cite_start]

It is measured in Watts (W) and represents the real energy transfer in a circuit[cite: 10].

1.d) Define the average value of a sinusoidal quantity.
[cite_start]

The average value of a sinusoidal quantity over a **half-cycle** is the arithmetic mean of all instantaneous values over that period[cite: 11].

[cite_start]

For a **full cycle**, the average value is **zero**[cite: 11].

1.e) What is a step-up transformer?
[cite_start]

A step-up transformer increases the input voltage from the primary winding to a higher output voltage at the secondary winding[cite: 13].

[cite_start]

It achieves this by having **fewer turns on the primary** than on the secondary winding[cite: 14].

1.f) Define the regulation of a transformer.
[cite_start]

Regulation is defined as the change in the secondary terminal voltage from no-load to full-load[cite: 16].

[cite_start]

It is expressed as a percentage of the full-load voltage and indicates the transformer’s ability to maintain a constant output voltage[cite: 16].

1.g) What type of material is used for the brushes of a d.c machine?
[cite_start]

Carbon or Graphite materials are primarily used for brushes in a d.c. machine[cite: 18].

1.j) What is the energy consumed by a 1000 watts heater in 3 hours?
[cite_start]

The energy consumed is 3 kWh (kilowatt-hours)[cite: 24].

Calculation: $\text{Energy} = \text{Power} \times \text{Time}$

[cite_start]

$$1000 \, \text{W} \times 3 \, \text{h} = 3000 \, \text{Wh} = 3 \, \text{kWh}$$ [cite: 25]
